Timeline of events

  • 1867: The British North America Act was passed and the Customs Department was created. This gave the newly formed Government of Canada the power to raise money through taxation. The federal government used only indirect taxes, such as customs duties and excise taxes, to raise the money it needed until 1917.
  • 1870: The federal government raised the taxes on liquor, beer, malt, cigarettes, cigars, and snuff, and imposed import duties on vinegar, wheat, and grain.
  • 1914: The high costs of World War I meant that the federal government had to make major changes to its tax system.
  • 1916: The Business Profit War Tax Act gave the federal government the ability to directly tax Canadian corporations if their profits were more than a certain percentage of their invested capital.
  • 1917: The federal government, led by Sir Robert L. Borden, introduced the Income War Tax Act. In referring to the Act, Sir Thomas White, Minister of Finance, said: "I have placed no time limit upon this measure… a year or two after the war is over, the measure should be definitely reviewed." In July of that year, the federal government imposed a general tax on corporate and personal income. Revenues from the tax were recorded in the 1916 to 1917 fiscal year and collected by the Commissioner of Taxation. The Commissioner was with the Department of Finance.
