Visual design principles

Visual hierarchy

Definition: Refers to the arrangement or presentation of elements in a way that implies importance.

In practice:

Visual hierarchy suggests there is a proper way to view content visually: in a hierarchical way. In other words, there is a pecking order to things…some content should be viewed first, some second, some third, and on down the line. The most important content is at the top of the hierarchy. It’s the visual element you look at first, which then directs you to what to look at next. Just like a great writer starts with a interesting lead that sends you breathlessly into the next paragraph, a thoughtful designer will efficiently move the viewer from one piece of content to the next.

The best visual hierarchies lead users to take the action confidently. They have a clear, obvious order in which to view and act on things, with the most important things first...Weak hierarchies, on the other hand, don’t set appropriate context. They may leave out something important, they may frame their topic incorrectly, or they may place design elements in the wrong order. Weak hierarchies fail to organize information in a helpful way and leave users without a clear idea of what to do or read next.

Visual Hierarchy - 52 weeks of UX (external link)
Joshua Porter

This is supported by:

Pareto principal (80/20 rule)

Definition: A high percentage of users perform a low percentage of actions. Most users go to a small percentage of pages and perform a small percentage of tasks.

In practice: Using analytics and research, identify the actions most users perform and put emphasis on those tasks. De-emphasize content that has low user usage.

Law of proximity

Definition: We perceive objects or shapes that are close together as forming groups. This is true even if the shapes, sizes and objects are very different.

In practice: Proximity is a way to connect similar content or distinguish different content. Group similar content together (close proximity) to communicate that it is part of whole. Add extra space to ensure content that is unrelated is perceived as separate.

This law is often neglected, as designers rely on the default padding and margin that come with the web design components (headings, paragraphs, buttons, bordered containers etc). Analyze each area of content with a critical eye. This ensures that the grouping or separation of the individual items creates the intended associations.

This is supported by:

White space

Definition: White or negative space is the empty portion of a page. It’s the gap between each element on the page.

In practice: White space is not lost or wasted space. It is important design element. In 1930 Jan Tschichold wrote "white space is to be regarded as an active element, not a passive background". In other words, each time we add an element, we remove value-added white space.

Law of similarity

Definition: We group similar things together. This similarity can occur in the form of shape, colour, shading or other qualities.

In practice: Users notice matching forms and shapes, colors and lines. Design components can work to enhance the content, and can also confuse it. Users perceive items with the same characteristics (similar elements) as related, even if this is not the intention.

Law of closure

Definition: We perceive forms and figures in their complete appearance despite the absence of one or more of their parts, either hidden or totally absent.

In practice: Users already close off blocks of content without the need to distinctly mark the perimeter. Don't over-use borders and background fill colours.

Law of symmetry

Definition: We perceive objects as being symmetrical (equal mirrored parts) and forming around a center point. Symmetrical elements are generally perceived as balanced, even numbered parts.

In practice: When there are two symmetrical elements, the mind perceives them as one connected, whole shape. This is typically seen in graphic design, rather than web design.

Law of common fate

Definition: We perceive objects as lines that move along a path. We group them together when they have the same direction of motion.

In practice: Objects with a path of movement are interpreted as one unified group. This can be used to guide the user’s attention.

Law of continuity

Definition: We perceive a line as an object that continues along the smoothest path. When objects cross over each other (for example lines) we perceive the two objects as two single lines, not four.

In practice: The eye has momentum as it moves from object to object. A grouping of content in a line, horizontally or vertically, increases this effect. Continuation is powerful, as it overrules many visual effects, including color. This is typically seen in graphic design, rather than web design.

Law of figure and ground

Definition: We perceive objects as either figures (distinct elements of focus) or ground (background, foreground or landscape on which the figures rest).

In practice: A background colour is perceived as separate from the foreground text. It presents the user with more than one thing to note on the page. A background colour may add visual interest or a focal point to the page. It can also overload the user, so don't over-use it.

Law of focal point

Definition: Whatever stands out visually (a point of interest, emphasis, or difference) is first to capture and hold the viewer's attention.

In practice: It is the first point of interest that grabs the user. The user then moves to other parts of the page. With the focal point, determine where it is, how many there are, and does it emphasize the correct thing.

Law of past experience

Definition: Objects will be grouped together if the user previously experienced them in that manner.

In practice: Using consistent and repeatable design patterns ensures the user experience is optimized. New designs are at risk of being incorrectly perceived by the user.

Interaction design principles

Principle of visibility

Definition: Users need to easily find and see ways to use the page. The options are visible and are not distracting them with unnecessary or redundant information.

In practice: A good design will not confuse or overwhelm the user with unnecessary alternatives.

Designers will naturally understand this as the basic hierarchy of a design. Visibility isn’t a new idea... The key is to prioritize... If we design by committee and attempt to make everything important, we will in effect emphasize nothing.

Taking Donald Norman’s Design Principles to Web Design (external link)
Patrick McNeil

Principle of feedback

Definition: User has to be informed of the outcome of their action immediately after it occurs.

In practice: The design needs to provide feedback mechanisms for events/actions for example, a change of state or errors. Feedback must be clear, concise, in plain language.

Principle of affordances

Definition: The visual design properties of an object should suggest how to use it.

In practice: Links need to look and behave like links, including their default, hover, active and visited states. Buttons needs to look and behave like buttons, including their default, hover, and active states. Images and icons that are clickable need to look like they are clickable. All page elements that have an action to them, must suggest/imply the action. The actions must match the user's expectations.

Principle of mapping

Definition: There should be a relationship between controls and their effect. The user can then simply recognize how to use the system rather than have to recall, like on a stove. This reduces reliance on memory which slows down ability to act. Recognition is faster than recall. For example, the vertical scroll bar on a page tells the user where they are on the page. As it is dragged down, the page moves down at the same rate.

In practice: Position controls so that their locations and behaviours correspond to the layout and behaviours. For example, paging forward is on the right, and paging back is on the left. Additionally, if direction is implied thought arrows, it matches the user's mental model of back/forward/up/down.

Principle of constraints

Definition: There should be restrictions built into the interaction to reduce user error.

In practice: Where possible, prevent user errors by:

  • Disabling functions that do not apply
  • Using appropriate controls to constrain inputs (for example, radio buttons, drop-downs)

Principle of consistency

Definition: Similar actions and similar elements should be used to accomplish similar tasks (this reduces error and improves learnability). Consistency applies across all system levels and includes both aesthetic and functional elements:

  • Application (consistency from screen to screen or page to page)
  • Device (consistency on desktop, tablet, mobile device)
  • Platform (consistency across the whole system such as internal or external site)
  • Industry (consistency within the government domain)

In practice: In addition to matching the user's expectations in terminology, layout, and interactions, the experience should be consistent throughout the entire process and between related pages.

Put your buttons in consistent places on all your windows, use the same wording in labels and messages, use consistent color scheme throughout, and any other consistent models that are applicable for you.

Reuse Principle in User Interfaces (external link) Vishal Mehta
