Content design principles: CRA design system

Use these rules to create web content that is easy to find, understand and use.

Filter by category

Title and description Topics

Consider the needs of the audience

  • Be aware of literacy and numeracy levels
  • Design for accessibilty and inclusiveness
  • Know about device, screen size and technology use
  • Stop writing for specialists
  • Create bilingual content
 Tag1, Tag3

Create scannable content

Titles, headings, lists and whitespace help people find what they are looking for

 Tag1, Tag3

Determine the right format

  • Know the different format types
  • Avoid frequently asked questions (FAQs)
 Tag1, Tag2

Determine user tasks

Then make the information tailored to their needs

 Tag2, Tag3

Ensure consistency and predictability

use a standardized approaches and patterns so that people can scan content

 Tag1, Tag3

Get to the point

Be comprehensive in what needs to be done and take out everything that doesn't help complete the immediate task

 Tag2, Tag3

Give enough time to read and use content

People who are stressed, have a motor or cognitive disability will often need more time to complete tasks before they time out

 Tag2, Tag3

Have a content strategy

So you don’t duplicate or produce contradictory information that will prevent users from finding the right answers

 Tag2, Tag3

Identify users’ keywords

This will help make the task intuitive and easy to understand

 Tag2, Tag3

Make images perceivable to all

People who can’t see need you to describe images for them in text

 Tag2, Tag3

Make it easy to find content in search

make sure your page title is clear in search results, people only look at a couple results on the top of the page to see if they found the right information

 Tag2, Tag3

Make videos perceivable to all

By providing captions and transcripts for your videos you make them accessible to people who can’t see and those who can’t watch them in certain environments - noisy spaces, slow internet

 Tag2, Tag3

Provide content at the point of need

Don’t send people searching elsewhere, and avoid FAQs. too much information will make it difficult for people to find answers

 Tag2, Tag3

Start with the most important information

people scan to find specific keywords, if they don’t see them, they may think this information does not exist

 Tag2, Tag3

Use data and research

People often have different perceptions about certain words; user research such as search terms and page feedback can help us reduce confusion between what government says and what people think it means

 Tag2, Tag3

Use effective content structure

  • Present information gradually through progressive disclosure
  • Write in complete blocks
  • Consider page length
  • Know when to alphabetize
 Tag2, Tag3

Use links effectively

  • Use links strategically
  • Carefully select related content
  • Write descriptive links
  • Focus on the task
  • Make sure that links work
  • Link correctly to non-government or internal-to-government content
  • Correctly capitalize links
 Tag2, Tag3

Use the right tone and voice

  • Address the person in page titles, links and navigation labels
  • Use "we" to refer to the Government of Canada
  • Use contractions
  • Distinguish between an obligation and a recommendation
  • Explain references to legislation
 Tag2, Tag3

Write in plain language

  • Avoid jargon, idioms, and expressions
  • Use abbreviations and acronyms correctly
  • Use short and simply sentences and paragraphs
  • Write in an active voice
  • Use verbs correctly
  • Check the readability score

Write useful page titles and headings

  • Create clear paths to information
  • Write useful headings
  • Make the page heading unique
  • Front-load keywords
  • Correctly capitalize headings
  • Avoid punctuation
  • Correctly structure headings
