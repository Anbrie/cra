Consider the needs of the audience: CRA design system

When planning web content, consider people's reading, numeracy and literacy levels, as well as accessibility needs.

Literacy levels

Research shows people with literacy challenges:

  • spend a lot of time trying to understand words that contain more than 2 syllables skip over large amounts of information when it contains many multi-syllable words, uncommon terms and long sentences
  • find it difficult to understand content written above grade 8 reading level

To support all users:

  • Check that content is easy to understand
  • Create scannable content
  • Get to the point
  • Start with the most important information
  • Write in plain language
  • Write useful page titles and headings

Numeracy levels

Research shows people with numeracy challenges:

  • find it easier to read numerals instead of words for numbers (for example, 5 rather than five)

Among our discoveries was that numerals often stop the wandering eye and attract fixations, even when they're embedded within a mass of words that users otherwise ignore.

  • Why do users fixate on numerals? Because numbers represent facts, which is something users typically relish...
  • How do users' eyes locate numerals while skipping past words? The shape of a group of digits is sufficiently different from that of a group of letters to stand out to users' peripheral vision before their foveal vision fixates on them. 2415 looks different than four even though both consist of 4 characters...

Digits enhance the scannability of web content. It's that simple.

Nielsen Norman Group
Show Numbers as Numerals When Writing for Online Readers

Accessibility

Making content accessible means a wide range of people can use it, including people with physical and cognitive disabilities

Some disabilities are well-known

For example:

  • deafness
  • blindness
  • mobility limitations
Some of disabilities are invisible

For example:

  • reading disorders
  • learning disorders like dyslexia
  • attention deficit disorders
  • memory disorders
  • anxiety
  • migraines
  • chronic pain
Some disabilities are temporary

For example:

  • aging
  • an accident or illness
  • life changes such as pregnancy

Many of these people can experience 1 or more disabilities.

Even people who do not identify as having a disability will experience some form of situational disability, such as:

  • trying to hear something in a loud location
  • trying to find information during a stressful event

    If I’m in the state of mind that I’ve been laid off and not financially secure, I would not necessarily read everything.

    April 6, 2020 usability study of Canada Emergency Response Benefit (CERB)

To support all users:

  • Make content accessible and inclusive
  • Build in accessibility from the start Canada.ca (cananda.ca)
  • Understand disabilities and impairments: user profiles (gov.uk)
  • Learn about how people with disabilities use the web: user stories (w3.org)

Inclusiveness

Be respectful of human diversity across gender, sexuality, social class, culture, age and ability.

Gender inclusive language

Gender-inclusive writing, which avoids references to gender whenever possible, is increasingly becoming standard practice. It may be useful or even essential to apply gender-inclusive guidelines when writing to the following audiences:

  • individuals whose gender is unknown;
  • non-binary individuals (that is, individuals who do not identify with either the masculine or the feminine gender);
  • a diverse group of people (so that no member of the group feels excluded).
Gender-inclusive writing (Translation Bureau)

  • People with disabilities are asking us to use respectful terms when writing and speaking about them or about issues that affect their lives. They are also asking that images chosen to portray them be respectful and not reinforce outdated stereotypes.

  • Choose words that do not reinforce cultural or racial stereotypes
  • When creating fictitious scenarios, visuals or other multimedia:
    • Avoid stereotypes in job roles
    • Choose names that represent diverse gender identities, abilities and cultures

Device, screen size and technology use

Not everyone has access to the same tools and technologies across Canada. What people use and what kind of access they have can depend on many factors including age, income and location.

Depending on context of use, people will view your content on different screen sizes.

Phone and tablet screens display less content than desktop and laptop screens. Readers who use small screens:

  • have to go through more steps to access the same amount of information
  • rely on their short-term memory to recall information that isn't visible on the screen
  • may miss important information if it is not presented closer to the top of the screen
Example: UX confusion on a small device
Context
COVID self-assessment tool page had promotional content about an App presented above the actual self-assessment tool.

The perceived call-to-action buttons appear to be the app download

The real call-to-action is the 'Start' button at the bottom of the page

Insights
Person who arrived on this page, though this was a page to download an app and left the page. They thought they could not do the assessment without downloading the app.
Why this is important
Person missed the self-assessment tool and was not able to complete their task.

There is no one-size-fits all solution. You need to understand who is using your information and under what circumstances.

Specialized audiences

Research shows that both specialized and general audiences prefer information written in plain language. This is true of many specialized fields including health and law.

Government experts often say that because they’re writing technical or complex content for a specialist audience, they do not need to use plain English. This is wrong.

Research shows that higher literacy people prefer plain English because it allows them to understand the information as quickly as possible.

People understand complex specialist language, but do not want to read it if there’s an alternative. This is because people with the highest literacy levels and the greatest expertise tend to have the most to read. They do not have time to pore through reams of dry, complicated prose.

gov.uk
Content design: planning, writing and managing content

When writing for a specialized audience, use plain language.

If you use terms only specialists are familiar with, consider including a simple explanation for general audiences.

You must always include a summary in plain language for:

  • people who aren't familiar with the subject
  • specialists who might be scanning the content to decide whether it's relevant to their task
